Question: Why does Hamlet hesitate to take revenge on Claudius?

Retrieved context:
[Hamlet, Act 3, Scene 1] HAMLET: "To be, or not to be, that is the question: Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer the slings and arrows of outrageous fortune, or to take arms against a sea of troubles..."
[Hamlet, Act 2, Scene 2] HAMLET: "The spirit that I have seen may be a devil, and the devil hath power t' assume a pleasing shape..."

Good answer:
Two retrieved passages help explain Hamlet's hesitation. In Act 3 Scene 1, Hamlet asks himself whether it is better to endure suffering or to act against it — this shows he is deeply uncertain about whether taking action is even the right thing to do. He is not just afraid; he is genuinely unsure whether revenge is morally justified. Then in Act 2 Scene 2, he raises another fear: that the ghost might actually be a devil in disguise, tricking him into committing murder. He says the spirit "may be a devil" with the power to take on a pleasing form. So even if he wanted to act, he does not fully trust the evidence. Together, these passages show that Hamlet delays because of both moral doubt and distrust of what he has seen.
